## The convenience premium is real

Finished items keep a price premium because they save buyers time, clicks and uncertainty. That can mean a clean utility craft, an upgraded enchant book, a forge output, or any item where the seller already did the annoying assembly work. In other words, you are not just selling an item. You are selling skipped friction.

That is why craft flipping fits so naturally between bazaar and AH flipping. You learn the basket cost like a bazaar player, but you also learn how finished goods behave on a player market where branding, convenience and account stage all affect what buyers will pay.

## Recipe reality comes before profit math

The most expensive craft mistakes happen before the sell begins.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Confirm the full path first">
If you assume a tier upgrade exists when it does not, if you forget a collection gate, or if you ignore ingredient volatility, the spreadsheet margin means nothing. Good craft flippers slow down long enough to confirm the full path from inputs to finished item.
</Callout>

This is why the strongest creator guides keep repeating craftability checks. It is not because the method is weak. It is because the method is strong enough that lazy players will skip the boring checks and pay for it later.

## Book ladders are the clearest teaching example

Enchant books show the upside and the danger of craft flipping better than any other niche. Low-tier books often look dull until they are combined into the version people actually want. But not every apparent ladder is valid and not every upgraded book moves quickly enough to justify the basket cost. The spread only matters if the buyer pool is real.

That same logic scales into other craft types. The best flips tend to sit close to a familiar friction point: a messy recipe, a multi-step combine or a task buyers would rather skip.
